About Me

Start Before You Feel Ready

My journey into technology did not begin with certainty. It began with curiosity, self-doubt, and the decision to start anyway.

Start Before You Feel Ready

I did not begin my journey into technology because I felt fully ready. I started because curiosity became stronger than self-doubt.

For a long time, I waited to feel more prepared before writing code. But confidence did not come from waiting. It started coming when I finally took action, made mistakes, fixed errors, and kept going.

From Literature to Code

Coming from a literature background, technology felt foreign and intimidating at first. I was used to words, ideas, analysis, and expression — not terminals, commands, files, or programming logic.

For months, I stayed in research mode. I watched tutorials, explored resources, and tried to understand where to begin, but I was not writing enough code.

Eventually, I realized that waiting to feel ready was only delaying my growth. So I started anyway. My first “Hello World” felt clumsy, but it was proof that I had moved from watching to doing.

Why Technology Pulled Me In

I became drawn to technology because I saw people around me building with it. I saw how ideas could become websites, tools, games, and systems that people could actually use.

What attracted me was not just code itself. It was the possibility of turning ideas into solutions, building something useful, and understanding how digital systems work from the inside.

I enjoy bringing solutions to problems, and technology gives me a practical way to do that.

The Project That Made It Real

One of the first projects that made technology feel real to me was ByteRush, an educational coding game I built and presented during my final project defense.

ByteRush pushed me to move from an idea to an actual project. I had to think about how the game should work, how users would interact with it, and how to present it clearly.

Presenting the project during my soutenance helped me see that building is not only about writing code. It is also about explaining your idea, defending your work, and improving how you communicate.

Because I come from an English-speaking background and presented in a French academic environment, the experience also challenged me to become more confident, adaptable, and clear in both languages.

How I Am Learning Now

Today, I learn by building, testing, documenting, and improving. I use GitHub to publish my projects, organize my work, and track my progress.

I also explore code-based learning platforms such as OverTheWire: Bandit and GameShell. OverTheWire: Bandit helps me practice Linux, SSH, file permissions, command-line navigation, and problem-solving. GameShell is a terminal-based learning game available on GitHub that helps me strengthen my command-line thinking in a more interactive way.

These challenges help me move beyond watching tutorials. They force me to think, test commands, make mistakes, debug, and understand what I am doing.

Building Across English and French

I am building this portfolio in both English and French because I want my work to be accessible across both languages.

Moving between English and French learning environments also helps me practice explaining technical ideas clearly to different audiences.

Where I Am Going

I am still growing, but I am building with intention. My goal is to become a strong software developer who can create useful, well-structured digital products and keep learning fast through real projects.

I am no longer only consuming technology. I am learning how to build with it.

Curiosity beats self-doubt when it turns into action.
